# 2.1 Introduction

This chapter presents a comprehensive review of the literature related to Agentic Artificial Intelligence (AI), Large Language Models (LLMs), Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), multi-agent systems, and their applications in healthcare. The purpose of this review is to establish the theoretical and technical foundation for the proposed Agentic AI framework for intelligent patient monitoring and clinical decision support.

Recent advances in large language models have significantly expanded the capabilities of artificial intelligence beyond traditional prediction tasks. Modern AI systems can now perform reasoning, planning, memory management, tool utilization, and autonomous task execution. These capabilities have led to the emergence of Agentic AI, where intelligent agents collaborate to solve complex problems through coordinated decision-making and interaction with external knowledge sources.

In the healthcare domain, AI has demonstrated promising results in disease diagnosis, clinical documentation, medical question answering, risk prediction, and treatment recommendation. At the same time, challenges such as hallucinated responses, limited reasoning transparency, fragmented patient information, and the lack of continuous patient context remain barriers to widespread clinical adoption. Researchers have proposed various techniques, including Retrieval-Augmented Generation, multi-agent collaboration, persistent memory, and explainable AI, to improve the reliability and trustworthiness of healthcare AI systems.

This chapter critically reviews influential research studies that have contributed to the development of Agentic AI and healthcare-oriented language models. It examines the evolution of LLMs, fundamental agent architectures, memory mechanisms, reasoning strategies, planning approaches, tool integration, and collaborative multi-agent frameworks. In addition, the chapter reviews recent healthcare AI systems that apply these concepts to clinical decision support and patient care.

The literature is analyzed not only to summarize existing research but also to identify limitations and research gaps that motivate the proposed framework. Particular attention is given to architectural components that support intelligent patient monitoring, including memory management, clinical reasoning, knowledge retrieval, agent orchestration, and trustworthy AI principles.

The chapter concludes with a research gap analysis that synthesizes findings from the reviewed studies and highlights the need for a unified Agentic AI framework capable of combining reasoning, memory, Retrieval-Augmented Generation, multi-agent collaboration, and Human-in-the-Loop validation for intelligent clinical decision support using the MIMIC-IV dataset.
