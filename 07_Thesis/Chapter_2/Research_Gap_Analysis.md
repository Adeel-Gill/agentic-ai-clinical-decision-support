# 2.9 Research Gap Analysis

The literature reviewed in this chapter demonstrates that significant progress has been made in Large Language Models (LLMs), Agentic AI, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), and AI-driven clinical decision support. Recent studies have introduced intelligent agent architectures capable of reasoning, planning, tool utilization, and collaborative problem solving [yao2023react; wu2024autogen; hong2024metagpt]. Healthcare-focused research has shown that LLMs can assist clinicians in medical question answering, diagnosis support, and clinical documentation [singhal2023clinical; tang2024medagents]. Despite these advancements, several important challenges remain unresolved, and it is worth being precise about what they are: the weakness of prior work is less about missing capabilities than about how narrowly those capabilities have been grounded and evaluated.

It is tempting to frame the gap as the absence of a unified framework that combines every component of Agentic AI into a single clinical decision support system. That framing is too weak to defend, because integration on its own is not novel — MedAgents, Agent Hospital, and comparable systems already combine memory, reasoning, planning, and multi-agent collaboration [tang2024medagents; li2024agenthospital]. The defensible gap lies in three specific places where even these integrated systems fall short: the data they are evaluated on, what their retrieval is grounded in, and whether verification and auditability are treated as first-class, measured components.

The first and most consequential shortcoming is evaluation on static examination question answering rather than longitudinal, real clinical records. MedAgents reports its gains on MedQA, MedMCQA, PubMedQA, and MMLU [tang2024medagents], and Agent Hospital, despite its evolving simulacrum of doctor and patient agents, ultimately benchmarks the acquired expertise on MedQA (USMLE) [li2024agenthospital]. AgentClinic moves toward interaction but with simulated rather than real patient trajectories [schmidgall2024agentclinic]. Examination questions are curated and self-contained; a real intensive-care admission is noisy, incompletely documented, and continually revised. High exam accuracy therefore offers little assurance that an agent can follow a deteriorating patient across days of MIMIC-IV observations [johnson2023mimic]. Effective patient monitoring requires continuous access to previous admissions, laboratory results, medications, diagnoses, and treatment history, which examination benchmarks do not exercise.

The second shortcoming concerns what retrieval is grounded in. Several studies address hallucination through Retrieval-Augmented Generation, yet most implementations retrieve from guidelines, textbooks, or literature rather than from the specific patient. MedRAG couples retrieval with a diagnostic knowledge graph but is oriented toward snapshot differential diagnosis and evaluated on DDXPlus and a chronic-pain dataset rather than a longitudinal ICU record [zhao2025medrag]. Med-PaLM encodes clinical knowledge parametrically and does not retrieve from a patient timeline at all [singhal2023clinical]. EHRAgent is the clearest step toward EHR grounding, executing code over structured records, yet it frames the task as answering discrete questions rather than maintaining a running, evidence-linked model of a patient over an admission [shi2024ehragent]. What is missing is retrieval anchored to the patient timeline, combining structured EHR evidence with medical literature so that patient-specific clinical evidence enters the decision.

The literature also exposes limits in reasoning reliability. Chain-of-Thought prompting, ReAct, and collaborative multi-agent reasoning improve decision quality [yao2023react; tang2024medagents], but hallucinated responses remain a considerable concern, and many systems still produce recommendations without verifiable evidence, which reduces clinician confidence and limits adoption.

A related structural issue is that many healthcare AI systems operate as standalone models rather than collaborative multi-agent environments. Complex clinical workflows span monitoring, diagnosis, risk prediction, treatment planning, and explanation generation, and a single model tends to perform some of these well and others poorly. Multi-agent collaboration for real-world clinical decision support therefore remains an emerging rather than an established practice [wu2024autogen; hong2024metagpt; tang2024medagents].

The third and most under-served shortcoming is trustworthiness, and specifically verification and auditability as first-class, evaluated components. Recent work emphasizes explainability, fairness, and safety [singhal2023clinical], but rarely includes a dedicated verification step that checks a generated recommendation against the retrieved patient evidence, and even more rarely measures whether the resulting audit trail is faithful to the evidence the system actually used. In a domain where a wrong recommendation can harm a patient, agent consensus is not equivalent to evidential entailment, and an audit trail that is merely produced is not the same as one whose faithfulness has been verified.

**Missing from prior work.** The comparative analysis in the accompanying matrix makes this concrete. When systems are scored on general capabilities — memory, planning, reasoning, multi-agent collaboration, healthcare focus, trustworthiness — the rows for ReAct, AutoGen, MetaGPT, MedAgents, Agent Hospital, and MedRAG collapse into nearly identical check marks, which is precisely why an integration-based claim to novelty is weak. When the same systems are scored on the five differentiating columns — patient-timeline RAG grounded in the EHR, a dedicated verification gate, longitudinal per-patient memory, evaluation on real ICU data (MIMIC-IV), and a faithful audit trail — no prior system carries more than a single check, and most carry none [tang2024medagents; li2024agenthospital; zhao2025medrag; shi2024ehragent; schmidgall2024agentclinic; johnson2023mimic]. Each prior system misses at least one of these dimensions, so the gap is real rather than rhetorical.

These observations point to the need for an Agentic AI framework that grounds retrieval in the patient's own longitudinal EHR timeline, coordinates specialized clinical agents, and treats verification and faithful auditability as evaluated components rather than aspirations. Such a framework should support intelligent patient monitoring while providing transparent, evidence-based recommendations that assist clinicians without replacing their judgment.

The proposed research addresses these gaps by designing an Agentic AI framework for intelligent patient monitoring and clinical decision support using the MIMIC-IV dataset [johnson2023mimic]. The framework combines longitudinal patient memory, ReAct-based reasoning [yao2023react], patient-timeline Retrieval-Augmented Generation over EHR data and medical literature, coordinated specialized agents, a dedicated verification gate, and Human-in-the-Loop validation to improve the quality, transparency, and reliability of AI-assisted clinical decision making. The architecture that realizes these components is presented in detail in Chapter 3.
