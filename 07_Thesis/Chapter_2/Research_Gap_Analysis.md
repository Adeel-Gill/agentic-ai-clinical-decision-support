# 2.9 Research Gap Analysis

The literature reviewed in this chapter demonstrates that significant progress has been made in Large Language Models (LLMs), Agentic AI, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), and AI-driven clinical decision support. Recent studies have introduced intelligent agent architectures capable of reasoning, planning, tool utilization, and collaborative problem solving. Similarly, healthcare-focused research has shown that LLMs can assist clinicians in medical question answering, diagnosis support, and clinical documentation. Despite these advancements, several important challenges remain unresolved.

One major limitation identified across the literature is the absence of a unified framework that combines all essential components of Agentic AI into a single clinical decision support system. Existing studies typically focus on individual capabilities such as reasoning, memory, planning, or retrieval mechanisms, while few integrate these features into a comprehensive architecture suitable for intelligent patient monitoring.

Another significant gap is the limited use of persistent memory for maintaining longitudinal patient information. Most healthcare LLM applications rely only on the current prompt or retrieved documents without preserving historical patient context. Effective patient monitoring requires continuous access to previous admissions, laboratory results, medications, diagnoses, and treatment history. Without persistent memory, AI systems cannot effectively support long-term clinical decision making.

The literature also highlights limitations in reasoning reliability. Although techniques such as Chain-of-Thought prompting, ReAct, and collaborative multi-agent reasoning improve decision quality, hallucinated responses remain a considerable concern. Many systems generate recommendations without providing verifiable evidence, reducing clinician confidence and limiting adoption in healthcare environments.

Several studies address hallucination through Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), yet most implementations retrieve information only from external knowledge sources. Few systems combine retrieval from both medical literature and structured Electronic Health Records (EHRs), limiting the ability to incorporate patient-specific clinical evidence into decision making.

Another important observation is that many existing healthcare AI systems operate as standalone models rather than collaborative multi-agent environments. Complex clinical workflows involve multiple specialized tasks, including patient monitoring, diagnosis, risk prediction, treatment planning, and explanation generation. A single language model often struggles to perform all these responsibilities effectively. Multi-agent collaboration remains an emerging research area with limited application in real-world clinical decision support systems.

Trustworthiness also remains a critical challenge. Although recent research emphasizes explainability, fairness, and safety, relatively few frameworks integrate these principles as core architectural components. Many proposed systems provide accurate predictions but lack transparent reasoning, confidence estimation, audit logging, verification mechanisms, and continuous human supervision. These capabilities are essential for healthcare applications where patient safety is the highest priority.

Furthermore, current research rarely demonstrates complete end-to-end architectures using publicly available clinical datasets such as MIMIC-IV. While MIMIC-IV has become one of the most widely used benchmark datasets for medical AI research, few studies utilize it to develop comprehensive Agentic AI frameworks that integrate reasoning, memory, retrieval, orchestration, and clinical decision support within a single architecture.

Based on these observations, there is a clear need for a comprehensive Agentic AI framework capable of integrating patient data management, persistent memory, Retrieval-Augmented Generation, multi-agent collaboration, clinical reasoning, and trustworthy AI principles into a unified system. Such a framework should support intelligent patient monitoring while providing transparent, explainable, and evidence-based clinical recommendations that assist healthcare professionals without replacing their clinical judgment.

The proposed research addresses these gaps by designing an Agentic AI framework for intelligent patient monitoring and clinical decision support using the MIMIC-IV dataset. The framework combines persistent memory, ReAct-based reasoning, Retrieval-Augmented Generation, coordinated specialized agents, and Human-in-the-Loop validation to improve the quality, transparency, and reliability of AI-assisted clinical decision making.
