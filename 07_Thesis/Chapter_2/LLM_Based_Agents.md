# 2.X Large Language Model (LLM)-Based Agents

The emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) has significantly changed the way artificial intelligence systems process information, generate responses, and interact with users. Traditional machine learning systems are generally designed for specific tasks and require task-specific training data. In contrast, modern LLMs demonstrate the ability to perform multiple tasks, including language understanding, reasoning, summarization, question answering, and decision support by utilizing knowledge learned from large-scale pre-training.

However, conventional LLMs primarily function as passive response-generation systems. They receive a user prompt, generate a response, and terminate the interaction. Although these models possess strong language understanding and generation capabilities, they lack several important characteristics required for autonomous intelligent systems, including persistent memory, long-term planning, interaction with external environments, and independent decision-making. These limitations have encouraged researchers to extend LLMs into more advanced architectures known as **LLM-based agents**.

LLM-based agents enhance traditional language models by integrating additional components such as memory systems, planning mechanisms, reasoning strategies, external tools, knowledge retrieval systems, and feedback loops. These components enable agents to perceive information, reason about available knowledge, perform actions, and continuously improve their decision-making capabilities.

A typical LLM-based agent architecture consists of three fundamental components: **brain, perception, and action modules**. The brain component represents the reasoning capability provided by the underlying language model. It enables task understanding, decision-making, and generation of intermediate reasoning processes. The perception component allows agents to collect information from external sources, databases, sensors, or user interactions. The action component enables agents to execute tasks through external tools, APIs, software systems, or interactions with other agents.

Recent studies have introduced several important approaches to improve the capabilities of LLM-based agents. The **ReAct (Reasoning and Acting)** framework combines reasoning traces with task-specific actions, allowing agents to iteratively analyze problems, interact with external environments, and update their plans. This approach improves the ability of LLMs to solve complex tasks requiring both reasoning and external information retrieval.

Similarly, **Generative Agents** introduced a memory-driven architecture that enables agents to maintain continuous records of experiences and observations. The memory stream allows agents to retrieve relevant historical information, perform reflection, and generate future plans. This capability is particularly important for healthcare applications, where patient information such as previous diagnoses, clinical history, treatment responses, and monitoring observations must be preserved over extended periods.

The development of **multi-agent frameworks** has further expanded the capabilities of LLM-based systems. Frameworks such as AutoGen, CAMEL, and MetaGPT demonstrate how multiple specialized agents can collaborate by assigning different roles, communicating information, and coordinating tasks. This approach closely aligns with healthcare environments where clinical decisions often require collaboration between different specialists, diagnostic systems, monitoring systems, and knowledge-based assistants.

Another major advancement in LLM-based agents is the integration of **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)**. RAG improves the reliability of language models by connecting them with external knowledge sources, including medical databases, clinical guidelines, research literature, and electronic health records. Instead of relying only on internal model parameters, RAG-based agents retrieve relevant information before generating responses. This reduces hallucination risks and improves the accuracy and reliability of generated recommendations.

In healthcare, LLM-based agents have demonstrated promising applications in medical question answering, diagnosis assistance, patient monitoring, clinical documentation, and decision support. Research systems such as MedAgents, MedRAG, Agent Hospital, and Med-PaLM-based models demonstrate how LLM capabilities can be adapted for medical reasoning and healthcare-specific tasks. These systems highlight the potential of combining medical knowledge retrieval, reasoning mechanisms, and specialized agents to support healthcare professionals.

Despite significant progress, several challenges remain before LLM-based agents can be safely deployed in real clinical environments. Healthcare applications require high levels of reliability, transparency, privacy protection, and regulatory compliance. Issues including hallucination, biased recommendations, incorrect reasoning, and lack of explainability remain major concerns. Therefore, trustworthy AI mechanisms such as human-in-the-loop validation, explainable recommendations, audit logging, and safety verification are essential for healthcare-focused agentic AI systems.

For the proposed research, LLM-based agents provide the foundation for developing an intelligent patient monitoring and clinical decision support framework. By integrating memory, reasoning, planning, retrieval mechanisms, and specialized healthcare agents, the proposed framework aims to overcome the limitations of traditional AI approaches and provide adaptive, explainable, and context-aware clinical assistance.
