# Chapter 4: Experimental Design and Evaluation

## 4.0 Preface: A Design, Not a Result

This chapter specifies how the proposed framework will be evaluated; it does not report outcomes. The thesis delivers an executable evaluation protocol rather than a completed empirical study, and the distinction matters for how the reader should judge the claims that follow. Where later chapters or a follow-on study report numbers, those numbers must come from running the procedure defined here without modification. Nothing in this chapter should be read as evidence that the framework works. It is instead an argument that the framework *can be tested fairly*, that each of its parts can be isolated, and that a skeptical reviewer would accept the resulting measurements as informative. I state this plainly because a common failure mode in agentic-AI papers is to blur the line between a proposed method and a demonstrated one, and the design thesis format is only honest if that line stays visible.

## 4.1 Evaluation Objectives Mapped to the Research Questions

The evaluation exists to answer the five research questions from Chapter 1, and each question demands a different kind of evidence. RQ1 asks what limitations current LLM-based healthcare systems exhibit; this is answered partly by the literature review and partly by the single-LLM baseline (B0) below, which reproduces those limitations under controlled conditions so the rest of the study has a concrete point of departure. RQ2 concerns whether agentic capabilities, that is, reasoning, planning, memory, and autonomous collaboration, actually improve behavior, and it is answered by the contrast between the flat and orchestrated configurations and by the memory ablation. RQ3 concerns multi-agent collaboration under explainability and physician oversight, and it maps onto the verification ablation, the explainability study, and the safety metric. RQ4 concerns whether Retrieval-Augmented Generation and external medical knowledge improve reliability and transparency, and it maps directly onto the faithfulness metric and the retrieval ablation. RQ5, the broadest, asks whether the framework supports intelligent patient monitoring on MIMIC-IV [johnson2023mimic], and it is answered in aggregate by the full task suite.

Framing the objectives this way forces a discipline that agentic-system papers often lack: every metric reported later must trace back to a research question, and every research question must have at least one metric that could, in principle, falsify a positive claim about it. A metric that cannot go against the framework is not a test. The hypotheses in Section 4.6 and in the companion protocol (`06_Experiments/Experimental_Design.md`) are written so that a null or negative result is a legible, reportable outcome rather than a failure of the study.

## 4.2 Cohort and Tasks Derived from MIMIC-IV

All patient data come from MIMIC-IV [johnson2023mimic], a de-identified critical-care database distributed through PhysioNet [goldberger2000physiobank]. Working within a single, well-documented source keeps the cohort reproducible and sidesteps the data-harmonization problems that would otherwise consume the project, though it also bounds external validity in ways Section 4.8 revisits.

The prototype evaluates on a bounded cohort of roughly 100 to 500 adult ICU stays rather than the full database. This is a deliberate scoping decision, not a shortcut, and it deserves justification because a reviewer will reasonably ask why the cohort is so small. Three reasons drive it. First, several of the framework's most important outputs, namely diagnosis support, treatment recommendation, explanation faithfulness, and safety, can only be scored by clinician chart review or by an LLM-as-judge protocol that is expensive per case; a five-figure cohort would make the clinician-in-the-loop metrics impossible to collect within a thesis timeline. Second, the end-to-end pipeline invokes multiple LLM agents per case, so cost and latency scale linearly with cohort size, and a bounded cohort keeps per-configuration cost in a range the project can actually afford to run several times for statistical stability. Third, a smaller cohort permits stratified sampling that guarantees adequate representation of the positive class for each prediction task, which matters because ICU mortality and sepsis onset are minority outcomes and a naive random draw would leave the risk-prediction analysis underpowered. The cost of this choice is honest and stated up front: confidence intervals will be wide, and the risk-prediction results in particular should be read as feasibility evidence rather than as competitive benchmarking against models trained on the full MIMIC-IV population.

The cohort is drawn by stratified sampling on outcome and on a coarse acuity measure so that each task has enough positive cases to estimate discrimination. Patient-level splitting prevents the same subject appearing in more than one fold, which would otherwise inflate every metric. Six tasks span the framework's clinical surface. Four are risk-prediction tasks framed as binary classification over a fixed prediction window: in-hospital mortality, ICU transfer or escalation, sepsis onset, and 30-day readmission. Sepsis onset uses a Sepsis-3-style operational definition [singer2016sepsis3], and the exact time anchors, inclusion rules, and censoring conventions are pinned in `06_Experiments/Experimental_Design.md` so the labels are reproducible. The fifth task is diagnosis support: given a structured and narrative snapshot of a stay, the framework proposes a ranked differential, scored against the recorded discharge diagnoses. The sixth is treatment recommendation: given the same snapshot, the framework proposes next clinical actions, scored for guideline concordance and safety. Diagnosis and treatment are harder to score objectively than the prediction tasks because the recorded chart reflects one clinician's path rather than ground truth, and the metrics in Section 4.5 are chosen with that limitation in mind.

## 4.3 Baseline Ladder

The central methodological commitment of this evaluation is that each architectural contribution must be isolated, not bundled into a single all-or-nothing comparison. A framework that wins only as a monolith teaches nothing about which of its parts earned the win. The baselines therefore form a ladder in which each rung adds exactly one capability over the rung below.

**B0, single LLM zero-shot.** The base model answers each task directly with no retrieval, no tools, no agent decomposition, and no verification. B0 establishes the floor and operationalizes the limitations discussed under RQ1: hallucinated evidence, no grounding in the patient timeline, and no safety check. It is the honest control that later rungs must beat to justify their added cost and complexity.

**B1, LLM with guideline-only RAG.** B1 adds retrieval over clinical practice guidelines and a medical knowledge base, but nothing else. The gap between B0 and B1 isolates the contribution of retrieval alone, which speaks to RQ4. Note that B1 retrieves guidelines but not the patient's own timeline; that second retrieval source is introduced later and ablated separately in Section 4.4, because conflating "retrieval helps" with "patient-specific retrieval helps" is a frequent overclaim.

**B2, MedAgents-style multi-agent without verification.** B2 adds multi-agent decomposition and collaboration in the style of MedAgents [tang2024medagents] but deliberately omits the Verification agent. The gap between B1 and B2 isolates the contribution of multi-agent reasoning on top of retrieval, addressing RQ2 and RQ3. B2 is the most important baseline for making an honest case, because a critic will suspect that a full agentic framework wins simply by being a multi-agent system; B2 removes that confound by giving the baseline the same collaborative structure and withholding only the safety layer.

**B3, the full proposed framework.** B3 is the complete framework of Chapter 3, with the Coordinator, all specialized agents, patient-timeline retrieval, persistent memory, and the Verification agent active. The gap between B2 and B3 isolates the value of verification and the framework's remaining components; when read together with the ablations, it attributes any improvement to specific mechanisms rather than to the system as a whole.

For the four risk-prediction tasks, all four LLM configurations are additionally compared against a simple clinical baseline: a logistic-regression model on standard structured features, reported alongside a severity-score comparator such as SOFA [vincent1996sofa]. This comparator is not a formality. Structured-feature logistic regression is a strong, cheap, well-calibrated baseline for ICU risk, and if the agentic framework cannot match it on AUROC and AUPRC then the framework has no business making risk claims regardless of how well it does on the language tasks. Including it guards against the temptation to celebrate a language model that loses to logistic regression.

## 4.4 Ablation Studies

Where the baseline ladder builds the framework up one capability at a time, the ablations tear the finished framework down one component at a time, holding everything else fixed at the B3 configuration. Four ablations target the components whose value is most contestable.

Removing the Verification agent (−Verification) measures the safety layer's contribution directly and is the mechanistic counterpart to the B2-versus-B3 gap. The expectation is that unsafe-recommendation rate rises when verification is removed; if it does not, the Verification agent is not doing useful work and the thesis should say so. Replacing patient-timeline retrieval with guideline-only retrieval (−patient-timeline RAG) tests whether grounding in the individual patient's history, rather than in generic guidelines alone, actually changes outputs; this isolates the patient-specific half of RQ4. Disabling persistent memory (−Memory) tests whether long-term patient context and reused reasoning traces improve longitudinal tasks such as readmission, and it is the sharpest test of the memory claim in RQ2. Finally, replacing the orchestrated Coordinator with a flat configuration (flat vs. orchestrated Coordinator), in which agents run in a fixed sequence with no dynamic delegation, tests whether orchestration earns its complexity or merely adds latency. It is entirely possible that flat orchestration performs comparably on these tasks, and a negative result here would be a useful, publishable finding about when coordination overhead is justified.

Each ablation is run across all six tasks so that a component's value can be reported per task rather than averaged into a single misleading number; verification may matter enormously for treatment recommendation and hardly at all for mortality prediction, and collapsing that into one figure would hide the interesting structure.

## 4.5 Metrics by Dimension

Metrics are grouped by the dimension they measure, and each dimension answers to a research question. Precise formulas, tooling, and computation details live in `06_Experiments/Evaluation_Metrics.md`; what follows is the rationale for each choice, because a metric without a rationale invites cherry-picking.

Task accuracy for diagnosis and clinical QA is scored two ways. On the MIMIC-derived diagnosis task, ranked differentials are compared against recorded discharge diagnoses using top-k accuracy and mean reciprocal rank. Because recorded diagnoses are an imperfect reference, this task is paired with a control on MedQA [jin2021medqa], a multiple-choice medical QA benchmark with unambiguous answer keys. MedQA cannot exercise the patient-timeline or monitoring machinery, so it is not the headline task, but it separates the base model's raw medical knowledge from the framework's ability to apply it, and a framework that helps on MedQA but not on the MIMIC diagnosis task, or vice versa, tells a story worth reporting.

Hallucination and factual grounding are measured by faithfulness: the fraction of atomic claims in a generated output that are traceable to retrieved evidence, computed in the RAGAS style [es2024ragas]. Each output is decomposed into individual claims, and each claim is checked for support against the evidence the framework actually retrieved for that case. This is the primary quantitative test for RQ4 and the most direct rebuttal to the hallucination concern raised in RQ1. A known weakness of automated faithfulness scoring is that it rewards claims that are grounded but wrong when the retrieved evidence is itself wrong; the protocol therefore reports faithfulness alongside, not instead of, task accuracy.

Risk prediction is scored by AUROC and AUPRC on each of the four binary tasks, reported against both the logistic-regression baseline and the severity-score comparator. AUPRC is reported explicitly because the positive classes are rare and AUROC alone flatters models on imbalanced data. Calibration is treated as a first-class trustworthiness metric rather than folded into discrimination, discussed below.

Safety is measured as the rate of guideline-violating or otherwise unsafe recommendations, computed on the treatment task and reported both before and after the Verification agent acts. The pre/post contrast is the point: it shows not only how safe the final output is but how much of that safety the verification layer supplied, which is exactly the evidence RQ3 needs. Adjudication of what counts as unsafe follows a written rubric and, for the treatment task, clinician review.

Explainability is assessed by a small clinician Likert study with three to five reviewers, who rate generated explanations on faithfulness, meaning whether the explanation reflects the actual basis for the recommendation, and on usefulness for clinical decision making. The sample of reviewers is deliberately small and I will not pretend otherwise; with n=3–5 the study can surface qualitative problems and gross differences between configurations but cannot support strong quantitative claims, so inter-rater agreement is reported and results are framed as indicative. This addresses the explainability half of RQ3 [rasheed2022explainable].

Trustworthiness combines two measures. Calibration is quantified by expected calibration error (ECE) [guo2017calibration] on the risk-prediction tasks, because a confidence score that does not track true probability is actively dangerous in a clinical setting. Audit-log completeness is scored as the fraction of decisions for which the framework produced a complete, replayable trace of retrieved evidence, agent actions, and verification outcomes; a system that cannot show its work fails the trustworthiness bar regardless of accuracy [jimenez2023trustworthy].

Runtime captures end-to-end latency per case and estimated cost per case for each configuration. These are not secondary. A framework that improves faithfulness by five points at ten times the cost and latency of B1 may be clinically impractical, and reporting cost honestly lets the reader weigh the accuracy gains against what they cost to obtain.

|---|---|---|---|
| Task accuracy | Top-k accuracy, MRR; MedQA accuracy (control) | Diagnosis; MedQA [jin2021medqa] | RQ1, RQ2 |
| Factual grounding | Faithfulness (claims traceable to evidence, RAGAS-style) | Diagnosis, treatment, QA | RQ4 |

## 4.6 Statistical Analysis

Because the cohort is bounded, statistical honesty is more important here than in a large study, not less. Every headline metric is reported with a 95% confidence interval obtained by bootstrap resampling over patients rather than over predictions, so that within-patient correlation does not shrink the intervals artificially. For the primary comparisons on the ladder, B2 versus B3 in particular, differences are tested with paired procedures on the shared cohort: DeLong's test for differences in AUROC [delong1988comparing], and McNemar's or bootstrap paired tests for the classification and faithfulness metrics [dietterich1998approximate]. Pairing exploits the fact that all configurations run on the identical set of cases, which materially increases power relative to unpaired comparisons and partly offsets the small cohort.

The study runs many comparisons across configurations, tasks, and metrics, and the risk of a false positive somewhere in that grid is real. Reported p-values for the confirmatory hypotheses are therefore corrected for multiple comparisons using a Benjamini-Hochberg false-discovery-rate procedure [benjamini1995controlling], and the confirmatory hypotheses are declared in advance in `06_Experiments/Experimental_Design.md` so that they cannot be reverse-engineered from the results. Exploratory comparisons are labeled as such and their p-values reported without correction but also without confirmatory weight. Given the cohort size, I expect several comparisons to be underpowered; where an interval is too wide to support a conclusion, the honest report is "inconclusive at this cohort size," and that outcome is planned for rather than hidden.

## 4.7 Comparison Methods and Related Benchmarks

Three recent systems anchor the framework against the state of the art and, equally usefully, expose what this evaluation does not attempt. AgentClinic [schmidgall2024agentclinic] evaluates diagnostic agents in a simulated interactive clinical environment where the agent must gather information through dialogue; it is a more demanding interaction model than the static-snapshot tasks used here, and its existence marks a clear direction the present static evaluation cannot reach. EHRAgent [shi2024ehragent] focuses on code-generating agents that answer questions by executing queries over structured EHR data; it overlaps with the framework's data-layer reasoning and offers a natural point of comparison for the structured-data tasks, though the present framework emphasizes clinical reasoning and safety over query synthesis. AMIE [tu2025amie] is a diagnostic dialogue system evaluated against clinicians under carefully constructed conditions and sets a high bar for conversational diagnosis; it is far larger in scope and resourcing than a thesis prototype, and citing it is partly a statement of what this work deliberately does not claim to match.

Positioning against these three is meant to be sobering rather than flattering. The proposed framework is not evaluated in an interactive simulator like AgentClinic, does not compete on query-synthesis breadth with EHRAgent, and does not run a clinician-comparison dialogue study at AMIE's scale. What it offers that these do not combine in one system is the pairing of multi-agent clinical reasoning with an explicit verification layer, patient-timeline grounding, and an auditable trust layer, evaluated with safety and calibration as primary rather than incidental outcomes. The comparison table in `06_Experiments/Experimental_Design.md` records which capability each related system exercises and which this evaluation covers, so the reader can see the boundaries at a glance.

## 4.8 Threats to Validity and Limitations

Internal validity is threatened chiefly by label noise and by the use of an LLM-as-judge for several metrics. The recorded discharge diagnoses and clinician actions in MIMIC-IV reflect one care pathway, not ground truth, so a framework output that differs from the chart may be wrong or may simply be a defensible alternative; the diagnosis and treatment metrics therefore carry irreducible reference-standard uncertainty, mitigated but not eliminated by clinician review on a subsample. Where an LLM scores faithfulness or safety, the judge shares failure modes with the systems under test, and the protocol guards against this only partially through rubric-based scoring and human spot-checks. A further internal threat is contamination: MedQA and published guidelines may appear in the base model's training data, which would inflate B0's apparent knowledge and shrink the measured value of retrieval; the analysis notes this and treats the MedQA control as a lower bound on retrieval's benefit rather than a clean estimate.

External validity is limited hard by the single-source, single-setting, bounded cohort. Results on 100 to 500 MIMIC-IV ICU stays [johnson2023mimic] from one hospital system say little about other institutions, non-ICU settings, pediatric or ambulatory populations, or prospective deployment, and nothing in this design supports a claim of clinical readiness. The cohort size also means the risk-prediction comparisons are feasibility evidence, not competitive benchmarking. Construct validity is threatened by the gap between what the metrics measure and what clinicians actually care about: faithfulness measures traceability to retrieved evidence, not correctness; a low unsafe-recommendation rate on a rubric is not the same as safety in practice; and an n=3–5 Likert study measures a handful of reviewers' impressions, not the usefulness of explanations at scale. Finally, the whole study evaluates a research prototype under retrospective, static conditions, which cannot capture the failure modes that appear only under live monitoring, streaming data, and real clinician interaction.

## 4.9 Summary

This chapter defined an evaluation protocol rather than reporting results, and its value rests on three commitments. First, every metric traces to a research question and can, in principle, return a negative verdict, so the design can disconfirm as well as confirm. Second, the baseline ladder from a single zero-shot LLM through guideline RAG and unverified multi-agent reasoning to the full framework, combined with component-wise ablations, isolates each contribution instead of testing the system as an indivisible whole; the B2-versus-B3 gap and the verification ablation together carry the framework's central claim that an explicit safety layer adds measurable value. Third, the design is honest about its limits: a bounded MIMIC-IV cohort, wide confidence intervals, imperfect reference standards, LLM-judge dependence, and a small clinician study together mean the eventual results will be feasibility evidence for the framework's mechanisms, not proof of clinical utility. The companion documents in `06_Experiments/` translate this design into reproducible, config-driven runs and pre-registered hypotheses so that the protocol can be executed exactly as specified. Chapter 5 draws out the conclusions this design is positioned to support and the future work required to move from a bounded prototype toward a clinically meaningful evaluation.
